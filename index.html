Can Malaysia Feed Itself?

Spoiler: it depends what's on your plate.

Your nasi lemak sambal? Imported chilli. Your rendang? Imported beef. Your teh tarik? Imported milk.
We ship palm oil to 150 countries - yet we can't grow enough shallots to season our own food.
Malaysia feeds the world. Just not always itself.

The Big Numbers

We eat a lot.
We import a lot more.

Every year, Malaysia's Department of Statistics tracks 60 food commodities - from rice and beef to chilli, shallots and everything in between. What they found might change how you think about the food on your plate.

What's On Your Plate?

Your chilli? Imported.
Your shallots? Very imported.

Self-sufficiency ratio (SSR) measures how much of what we consume is actually grown here. For some foods the answer is reassuring - for others, it's alarming.

Who's Self-Sufficient and Who Isn't?

SSR above 100% means we grow more than we eat. Below 100% means we depend on imports to fill the gap.

How Self-Sufficient Is Each Food Category?

Average SSR across all commodities in each category - fruits and vegetables perform well, but livestock is dangerously dependent on imports.

💡

With an average self-sufficiency ratio well below 100%, nearly every piece of meat and glass of milk on a Malaysian table has crossed an international border to get there. Fisheries sit in the middle ground, partly self-sufficient but increasingly reliant on imports as local catches decline.

Out of Every 100 Units Consumed, How Many Are Local?

Each square represents 1% of consumption. Green squares are locally produced, red squares are imported.

The pattern is clear: the closer a food is to the soil, the more likely Malaysia grows it. The closer it is to an animal, the more likely it came from abroad.

The Import Problem

We export palm oil.
We import everything else.

Malaysia is a major agricultural exporter — but mostly industrial crops, not food. Meanwhile, the foods Malaysians eat daily flow in from Thailand, Australia, India and beyond.

Import vs Export — Who's Winning?

Each bubble is a food category. Higher up = more imports. Further right = more exports. Bigger bubble = larger trade gap.

Malaysia's Growing Import Appetite

Food import quantities by category from 1961 to 2022 — every category is trending upward.

Palm Oil: Malaysia's Biggest Export — By Far

The gap between Malaysia's oil exports and imports has grown every decade. While we import almost no oil, we export billions of tonnes — mostly palm oil destined for the world.
